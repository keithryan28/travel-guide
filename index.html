Killarney

Killarney is arguably the most quintessential tourist destination in Ireland. Nestled at the foot of Ireland’s highest mountains and adjoining the beautiful Lakes of Killarney, the town is an essential stop over for anyone visiting the Emerald Isle.

The population of the town is 12,750 and its people are one of the friendliest you will ever meet.

Although primarily a tourist town, Killarney has not lost its charm. A plethora of restaurants, pubs, shops and music venues makes it the ideal centre for relaxation after a day’s touring around the most magnificent scenery.

Its name in Gaelic is Cill Airne, which means Church of the Sloes. Killarney was completely covered by an ice sheet during the Ice Age and this sculpted the magnificent mountains and lakes.

Killarney Attractions

  • Killarney National Park

    Ireland's most famous drive.

    Killarney National Park (Irish: Páirc Náisiúnta Chill Airne) is located beside the town of Killarney, County Kerry, Ireland. It was the first national park established in Ireland, created when Muckross Estate was donated to the Irish state in 1932.

  • The Skelligs

    Ireland's most famous drive.

    The Skellig Islands, once known as the Skellocks, are two small, steep, and rocky islands lying about 13 km west of Bolus Head on the Iveragh Peninsula in County Kerry, Ireland. They are famous for their thriving gannet and puffin populations, and for an early Christian monastery that is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

  • St. Marys Cathedral

    Ireland's most famous drive.

    St. Mary's Cathedral, Killarney, is a Roman Catholic cathedral. St. Mary's Cathedral (1842-1855) was designed by the renowned English Architect Augustus Welby Pugin and is considered to be one of the most important and best Gothic Revival churches of the nineteenth century in Ireland. The spire and nave were completed by the Irish Architects Ashlin and Coleman of Dublin.

  • Killarney Lakes

    Ireland's most famous drive.

    The Lakes of Killarney are a renowned scenic attraction located near Killarney, County Kerry, in Ireland. They consist of three lakes - Lough Leane, Muckross Lake (also called Middle Lake) and Upper Lake. Lough Leane (from Irish Loch Léin, meaning "lake of learning") is the largest of the three lakes.

Kenmare History

Kenmare derives its name from the old Gaelic name of Ceann Mara, meaning Head of the Sea. However, today it has the beautiful Gaelic name of An Neidin, meaning the Little Nest. This reflects the fact that the town lies between the two mountain passes The Caha Pass and Moll’s Gap.

With a population of approximately 2000 people, this figure swells during the tourist season. Known for its beautiful setting between the two passes, Kenmare is a must-stop place when travelling between Cork and Killarney.

Night-time entertainment during the summer is every bit as good as its larger neighbour Killarney, but does not feel as crowded with tourists.

Kenmare Activities

  • Moll's Gap

    Stunning scenic views

    Moll's Gap is a pass on the N71 road from Kenmare to Killarney in County Kerry Ireland. On the Ring of Kerry route, with views of the Macgillycuddy's Reeks mountains, the area and its shop is a panoramic spot visited by thousands of tourists each year. The rocks at Moll's gap are formed of Old Red Sandstone. Moll's Gap is named after Moll Kissane.

  • Beara Peninsula

    Ireland's rugged coastline

    Beara (Irish: Béarra) or the The Beara Peninsula is a peninsula on the south-west coast of Ireland, bounded between the Kenmare "river" (actually a bay) to the north side and Bantry Bay to the south. It has two mountain ranges running down its centre: the Caha Mountains and the Slieve Miskish Mountains. The northern part of the peninsula from Kenmare to near Ardgroom is in County Kerry, while the rest forms the barony of Bear in County Cork.

  • Druids Circle

    From Ireland's mythical past

    On a small hill near the central town market, this large Bronze Age druid stone circle is magnificently intact, with 15 standing stones arranged around a central boulder, which still bears signs (circular holes, a shallow dent at the center) of having been used in ceremonies. To find it, walk down to the market square and follow signs on the left side of the road. There's no visitor center, and no admission fee; it's just sitting in a small paddock, passed by the sleepy traffic of everyday life.

  • Carnegie Library

    Historical building

    Kenmare Carnegie Library was built in 1915. The lending library is located on the ground floor with Kerry County Council offices on the 1st floor. The original Carnegie Library building has been extended to the rear to accommodate Kenmare Arts Centre and theatre. The theatre space can also be used as a conference and exhibition centre. Renovation work was carried out in 1989, and further restoration and extension works in 2006.

Dingle History

The Gaelic name is Daingean Ui Chuis and it means the Fortress of Hussey, a Flemish family from the 13th Century. The town has a population of approximately 1,900 which is considerably swelled by tourists during the season. Set in the heart of the Gaeltacht (a region where Gaelic is the first language), Dingle people are bilingual and speak English fluently.

Dingle/An Daingean is a charming fishing town and boasts over 50 pubs, numerous restaurants and shops to die for. The people are extremely friendly and it is equally popular as a tourist destination for overseas visitors and native Irish alike. Fungi the Dolphin has been swimming with the boats in the harbour since 1984. The town has a wonderful aquarium a brewery and there is a seal sanctuary on the outskirts of the town.

The town is the perfect location from which to explore the magnificent Dingle Peninsula and Slea Head Drive as well as the perfect place to unwind after exploring the area.

The first people arrived here about 6,000 years ago. Remains of cattle bones dating back 5,000 years are the oldest evidence of cattle in Ireland. 3,200 BC, Beehive huts are built in the area. Built by Stone Age farmers, they were dwellings constructed of stone without any cement or mortar. They were later used by Monks as cells during the Middle Ages. 2,500 BC, Over 90 stone structures built in the Loch a’Duin valley.

Dingle Attractions

  • Gallarus Oratory

    Early Christian church

    The Gallarus Oratory is believed to be an early Christian church located on the Dingle Peninsula, County Kerry, Ireland. The oratory overlooks the harbour at Ard na Caithne on the Dingle Peninsula.

  • Reask Monastic Site

    Early Monastic Site

    Reask (Irish: Riasc) is a ruined early Monastic site located 1 km east of Baile an Fheirtéaraigh, County Kerry, Ireland. Although nothing remains of the buildings but low walls and a cross-slab standing stone which sits in the middle of the compound, this site gives a very good idea of the layout of a small monastery of the Early Medieval period.

  • Caherconree Hillfort

    Hillfort Ruins

    Caherconree is named after a stone ringfort that sits two-thirds of the way up its southwestern shoulder, overlooking the mountain road called Bóthar na gCloch ("road of the stones"). The ringfort is surrounded on three sides by steep cliffs. In Irish mythology this is the fort of Cú Roí mac Dáire, who was able to make it spin around at night to stop any attackers from finding the entrance.

  • Blasket Islands

    Formerly inhabited islands

    The Blasket Islands (Na Blascaodaí in Irish - etymology uncertain: it may come from the Norse word "brasker", meaning "a dangerous place") are a group of islands off the west coast of Ireland, forming part of County Kerry. They were inhabited until 1953 by a completely Irish-speaking population, and today are part of the Gaeltacht. The inhabitants were evacuated to the mainland on 17 November 1953. Many of the descendants currently live in Springfield, Massachusetts, and some former residents still live on the Dingle Peninsula, within sight of their former home.

    New local Brewery

    Nestled at the base of the majestic Conor Pass in the old Creamery building, the Dingle Brewing Company sits on Spa Rd,just on the outskirts of the picturesque village of Dingle, Co.Kerry,Ireland. Come sample our fresh blends of alcoholic brverages.

    Walk back in time . . . discover 500BC Dún Beag Fort and savor the spectacular sea views. Dún Beag Fort is an impressive and elaborate example of a promontory fort. Its location makes it one of the most dramatic archaeological sites on the Dingle Peninsula.

Cork History

Cork is the Republic of Ireland’s second largest city and third on the Island. It has a population of 118,912. County Cork is the largest County in Ireland. The Gaelic name for Cork is Corcaigh, meaning a marshy place. Cork is known as the Rebel County. Cork once had the world’s largest tractor factory owned by the Ford Motor Company. Cork was the world’s largest exporter of butter until recent times. Cork Harbour is the world’s second largest natural harbour. Middleton Distillery has the world’s largest Pot Still.

Founded in the 6thCentury by Saint Finbarr. 915 the Vikings arrive and set up a city. 1173 the Vikings of Cork attack the Norman invaders with a fleet of 32 ships, but are defeated. 1185 Prince John of England grants the city a Charter. 1349 half of the city’s population die of the Bubonic Plague. 1491 Perkin Warbeck, Pretender to the English Throne persuades the Mayor and other senior citizens of Cork to assist him in rebellion against Henry V11 giving Cork its title of the Rebel City. 1682 William Penn sets sail from Cork and founds Pennsylvania. 17th and 18th Centuries, Huguenots from France arrive seeking freedom from religious persecution in France. 1720, Royal Yacht Club set up.

First Yacht Club in the world. 1847 William Ford, the father of Henry Ford emigrates from Cork 1849 University College Cork opens. 1912. Titanic makes its final stop at Queenstown (now Cobh) in Cork Harbour before sailing to its doom. 1915 Lusitania is sunk off the Old Head of Kinsale, many bodies wash ashore in Cork Harbour. 1919 Henry Ford opens a tractor factory in Cork, his first venture outside the US. 1920 Tomas MacCurtain, Lord Mayor of Cork murdered by Black and Tans. His successor, Terence McSwiney is arrested and dies in prison after 76 days on hungerstrike. 1920 Black and Tans set fire to over 300 buildings in Cork City. July 1922, Anti Treaty forces seize City during Civil War and set up the “Republic of Munster”. 1922 Michael Collins, born in Clonakilty in Cork is killed at Beal na mBlath near Bandon County Cork. He was 32 years old. 1984 Ford and Dunlop factories close down.

Kinsale History

Kinsale comes from the Gaelic words, Cionn tSaile meaning the Head of the Saltwater. It is known as the Gourmet Capital of Ireland, Kinsale’s narrow streets enclose a plethora of fine food establishments making it a “Mecca” for food lovers from all over Ireland and the world.

A cosmopolitan town with Restaurateurs from all over the world, Kinsale has a distinctly Mediterranean feel and has an exceptionally temperate climate. Kinsale is both a major fishing village as well as a centre for pleasure boating and boasts a large marina. Nearby is Old Head of Kinsale Golf Club. One of Ireland’s most famous golf clubs, it is a must for discerning golfers.

Kinsale Attractions

  • King Charles Fort

    Historic Old Fort

    Charles Fort is one of the finest surviving examples of a 17th Century star-shaped fort, and much of the construction begun in 1678 remains. The fort has two enormous bastions overlooking the estuary, and three facing inland. Within its walls were all the barracks and ancillary facilities to support the fort’s garrison. The fort continued in military use until 1922.

  • Desmond Castle

    Museum

    Built as an urban tower house by the Earl of Desmond c. 1500, Desmond Castle has served as Customs House, Prison, Ordnance store and a Workhouse. The Museum details the history of the Wine Geese; the intriguing story of Ireland’s wine links with Europe and the wider world. This is an essential half-hour all-weather activity. (Photo: John Collins.)

Waterford History

The city was founded in 914. Reginald’s Tower, one of the attractions of the Viking Triangle, was built in the 10th Century on top of a older Viking tower. It is the oldest civic structure in Ireland and the first to be constructed using mortar. Waterford was captured by Strongbow (Richard de Clare, Earl of Pembroke), the first Norman invader on the 25th August 1170 and it is in Reginald’s Tower that he married Aoife, the daughter of Diarmuid Mc Murrough, the man who invited the Normans to Ireland.

In 1171, King Henry 11 of England arrives in Ireland to assume his control of the Normans in Ireland. In 1497, King Henry V11 grants Waterford its motto after successfully repulsing attacks by Perkin Warbeck and Lambert Simnell, two Pretenders to the Throne who were backed by the Irish Earls. In 1650, the city surrendered to Henry Ireton, Oliver Cromwell’s son-in-law.

The Iconic brand Waterford Crystal was founded In 1783, by George and William Penrose. Almost 70 years later it closed down, but is resurrected again in 1947. Up until it's closure again in 2009 the brand see's a meteroric rise in popularity and although it has ceased trading, it now has a centre in the viking triangle of waterford; containing showrooms and glass cutting demonstrations.

Between 1850 and 1860, the Malcomson family built and operated the world’s largest fleet of Iron Steam Ships. In 1848, Thomas Francis Meagher, on of the leaders of the Young Irelanders, unfurled a tricolour for the first time. Based on the French flag, it formed the basis for Ireland’s National Flag. A monument of Thomas Francis Meagher can be seen outside Reginalds Tower.

Dublin History

Dublin City is Ireland’s largest city with a population of 1,270,603. The Greater Dublin area has a population of 1.8 million, meaning that almost 25% of Irish people are located in this area. The Gaelic name for Dublin is Baile Atha Cliath, which means “The Town at the Ford of the Hurdles. The name Dublin is derived from the name Dubh Linn, meaning the Black Pool, which refereed to a deep lake over which Dublin Castle now lies. The Vikings referred to it as Dyflinn which became Dublin. A 4,000 year old prehistoric burial site where the Phoenix Park is situated is the earliest known inhabited site. The first settlements go back as far as 140AD when it was known as Eblana, but the city developed as a Viking town from 841 onwards. Dublin was for many years the second city of the British Empire and was the only city to be truly conquered by the English. So much so, that Dubliner’s are referred to as Jackeens (little John’s), a reference to John Bull, the nickname for the English. The city is a vibrant cosmopolitan city with historic buildings, great restaurants and pubs and Dublin people are renowned for their wit and humour.

    Historical College

    Trinity College (Irish: Coláiste na Tríonóide), formally known as the College of the Holy and Undivided Trinity of Queen Elizabeth near Dublin, is the sole constituent college of the University of Dublin in Ireland. The college was founded in 1592 as the "mother" of a new university,modelled after the collegiate universities of Oxford and of Cambridge, but, unlike these, only one college was ever established; as such, the designations "Trinity College" and "University of Dublin" are usually synonymous for practical purposes.

    Collins Barracks (Irish: Dún Uí Choileáin) is a former military barracks in the Arbour Hill area of Dublin, Ireland. The buildings are now the National Museum of Ireland, Decorative Arts and History. Housing both British armed forces, and Irish army garrisons through three centuries, the barracks were the oldest continuously occupied example in the world. Originally called simply The Barracks, and later The Royal Barracks, the name was changed to Collins Barracks when handed over to the Irish Free State in 1922.

    Museum

    The Dublin Writers Museum was opened in November 1991 at No 18, Parnell Square, Dublin, Ireland. The museum occupies an original 18th-century house, which accommodates the museum rooms, library, gallery and administration area. The annexe behind it has a coffee shop and bookshop on the ground floor and exhibition and lecture rooms on the floors above. The Irish Writers' Centre, next door in No 19, contains the meeting rooms and offices of the Irish Writers’ Union, the Society of Irish Playwrights, the Irish Children's Book Trust and the Irish Translators' & Interpreters' Association.

    Landmark

    Prior to the establishment of Glasnevin Cemetery, Irish Catholics had no cemeteries of their own in which to bury their dead and, as the repressive Penal Laws of the eighteenth century placed heavy restrictions on the public performance of Catholic services, it had become normal practice for Catholics to conduct a limited version of their own funeral services in Protestant churchyards or graveyards. Wicklow is Ireland’s newest county. The name Wicklow comes from the Norse “Vikingr Lo” meaning Viking Meadow. The Irish name Cill Mhantain means the Church of the Toothless One. Wicklow is Ireland’s newest County only being created in 1606 from parts of Dublin and Carlow. Wicklow Head is the most eastern part of the Republic of Ireland. Meath History

The Boyne Valley is situated in the County of Meath. Known as the Royal County, it was the seat of the High Kings of Ireland for many centuries. Meath comes from the Irish “An Mhi”, meaning “the middle”. During Celtic times Meath was a Province in its own right and was one of the five Provinces (Today there are just four). Indeed the Irish word for Province is “Cuige” meaning a fifth. The name Boyne Valley refers to the River Boyne and its surrounds. The river gets its name from the Irish Princess Boinne who drowned in it. The Boyne Valley is akin to Egypt’s Valley of the Kings and dates right back to the birth of Ireland and is the setting for some of the most important events in Irish History.

Loughcrew: The largest collection of Passage Tombs in the world. Dating back some 6,000 years ago, they are reputed to be the oldest manmade structures in the world. From the lofty height, it is said that on a clear day one can see 17 of the 32 Counties of Ireland.

Newgrange: It is the best preserved and finest Passage Tomb in the world. Built some 5,000 years ago, it is 500 years older than the Pyramids of Egypt and 1,000 years older than Stonehenge. Very little is known about the people who built it and as to who is buried within the tomb. Originally discovered in the 1699, it wasn’t until 1962 that excavation and restoration took place under the guidance of Professor Michael O’Kelly and his wife Claire. The O’Kelly’s discovered the unique feature of the tomb, a light box or opening above the entrance. At the Winter Solstice (December 21st) each year, the rising sun penetrates through the opening lighting up the interior of the grave. This remarkable sight only occurs at the Solstice and has being doing so for 5,000 years, making Newgrange the oldest solar observatory. The ceiling was built as a “corbel” ceiling, i.e. stones placed on top of one another without the use of mortar and yet the tomb has remained completely dry for the whole 5,000 years. A stop at the Visitor Centre, known as Bru na Boinne is a must with its audio-visual display, museum and the not to be missed guide of the interior of the tomb itself.

Knowth: Adjacent to Newgrange and part of the Bru na Boinne experience are the Passage Tombs of Knowth. The site dates from a similar period as Newgrange, but unusually it has two large Passage Graves. The site has also numerous satellites or smaller graves and was still in use until about 1400. Famed for the fact that it has the greatest concentration of Megalithic Art in the world. As with Newgrange, the site can only be accessed via Bru na Boinne.

Hill of Tara: Tara was the Seat of the High Kings of Ireland for many centuries and was in use up to the 11th Century. On a clear day one can see half the Counties of Ireland. There is not much left of this ancient site as it would originally have had wooden buildings. Nonetheless it still evokes a mystical and regal feeling amongst anyone visiting here. There is a Stone Age Passage tomb and two circular Iron Age Hill Forts. Within one of these forts (Cormac’s House), you will find the “Lia Fail” or Stone of Destiny. An ancient phallic symbol, it is said to give a loud roar when the true King passes it. The Mound of the Hostages refers to the Celtic custom of taking hostages to ensure the loyalty of vassal Kings. In the event that Tara was attacked, the hostages would be taken into the mound for execution if the opposing army did not withdraw.

Hill of Slane: On the Hill of Slane nearby Tara, St. Patrick is reputed to have lit the Paschal Fire. Tradition has it that the High King would light a fire in Tara on the feast of Bealtaine and no other fires should be lit. St. Patrick however wished to celebrate the Easter Vigil and so lit his own fire. Enraged, Laoghaire, the High King, sent his soldiers over to Slane to arrest Patrick and bring him back to Tara. Patrick tried to explain why he was building the fire and in the process, whilst rying to explain the Blessed Trinity, plucked a shamrock from the ground and a symbol of Ireland was born. The Paschal Fire lit by St. Patrick heralded the arrival of Christianity to Ireland.

Belfast History

Belfast is Ireland’s second largest City and the capital of Northern Ireland. The City has a population of 280,892 with the Greater Belfast area having a population of 641,638 which is half of the entire population of Northern Ireland. Northern Ireland is made up of 6 of the 9 Counties of the Province of Ulster. The City is divided between Catholics on the west and Protestants on the east. Belfast is the site of the Northern Ireland Assembly, originally the Northern Ireland Parliament, which is situated at Stormont Castle on the east of the City. The Irish for Belfast is Beal Feirste, meaning “Mouth of the Sandbank”.

Derry History

Derry has a population of 83,652 in the City and 105,066 in the urban area. It is the second largest city in Northern Ireland and the fourth largest on the island. It gets its name from the Irish “Doire” meaning Oak Grove and is one of the longest inhabited places in Ireland. Derry is the UK City of Culture for 2013 and was voted the 4th best city in the world to visit in the “Lonely Planet Guide”. It is the only remaining walled city in Ireland.

Enniskillen is the County town for the County of Fermanagh. The name comes from the Irish “Inis Ceithleann”, meaning Cethlenn’s Island. Cethlenn was an Irish goddess who drowned after being wounded in a battle and who attempted to swim the river. The County of Fermanagh is the only county in Northern Ireland that does not border Lough Neagh. Originally it was the fiefdom of the Maguires. The town occupies an island between Upper and Lower Lough Erne.

Galway is derived from the Irish Gaillimh which means a stony river. It is Ireland’s 5th largest city and third in the Republic. The population is 75,414. A University town as well as a maritime city, it has a vibrancy and cosmopolitan air that is a magnet for tourists and natives alike. Clifden History

Clifden is known as the capital of Connemara. Its name in Irish is An Clochan, meaning “Bee-Hive Cell” and comes from these early dwellings which were later used by Monks as Cells. Clifden has a population of 2,609. Connemara gets its name from Conmacne Mara, meaning the Conmacne tribe who lived by the sea. The population of Connemara is 32,000 of which almost 24,000 are native Irish speakers making this Ireland’s largest Gaeltacht. Connemara is breathtaking in its scenery, bordered by the Twelve Ben Mountains on one side and the Atlantic Coast on the other. It is famous for the Connemara Pony which is said to be descended from horses washed ashore from the Spanish Armada. Clifden has many artisan style shops.

It is 16 kilometres long and in the centre over 45 metres deep. It is one of three glacial fjords that exist in Ireland, the others being Lough Swilly and Carlingford Lough.

Can you confirm the power supply
requirements for our equipment in
Ireland.

  • The electrical supply in Ireland is 230v 50hz. The plugs and sockets are different from the USA involving a three-pronged formation, the same as those used in the United Kingdom. If your appliances operate on a different current (such as those from North America) you will need a power converter and plug adapter.
    A plug adaptor does not change the electricity supplied to the appliance, only allows it to be plugged into a different type of wall socket. If the appliance you are using supports dual voltage and dual frequency then a plate/tag will be located on the item stating “120/240v, 50/60Hz”.
    Most laptop computer and battery chargers are dual voltage, so all you will need to use them with a different supply is a plug adaptor.
    Power converters step down the voltage from 240v to 120v, allowing equipment which is not dual voltage to operate at the voltage for which it was designed. Converters do not alter the frequency at which electicity is delivered and should be used a maximum of 1-2 hours at a time.
    Converters can be purchased at travel stores, some discount stores, office supply stores, and electronics stores. Make sure that you select a converter that will accommodate the wattage of the appliances you wish to operate. Some laptop computers, electric razors and hair dryers have built-in power converters.
